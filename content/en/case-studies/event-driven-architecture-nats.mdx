title: "Event-driven architecture using NATS"
description: "Decoupling the social feed from live tournament operations — and being honest about what's actually wired versus just declared."
date: 2025-03-19
tags: ["NATS JetStream", "Event-Driven Architecture", "Backend"]

---

<Callout type="problem">
The social feed (posts, follows, player activity) is a separate service (`social-bff`) from the core tournament backend, and it shouldn't be able to slow down or break match scoring, registrations, or tournament automation — but it still needs to know when a match finishes or a player registers, reliably, not just "usually."
</Callout>

<Callout type="alternatives">
A direct HTTP call from the backend to `social-bff` on every relevant action was the simpler alternative, but it would couple the two services' uptime together — if `social-bff` (or its database) were slow or down, match-finish requests would inherit that latency or failure. NATS JetStream was chosen specifically over plain NATS because `social-bff` needs at-least-once delivery with replay (a message published while it's down shouldn't be lost), which plain pub/sub doesn't give you.
</Callout>

<Callout type="decision">
One JetStream stream (`UFT_EVENTS`) with a subject convention (`uft.<domain>.<event>`, wildcard-subscribed as `uft.>`), 7-day retention. `backend-nest` publishes fire-and-forget: every publish call is wrapped so that if NATS is unreachable, it's logged and swallowed — a NATS outage must never break the request that triggered it. `social-bff` consumes through a durable, named JetStream consumer with explicit ack/nak, so it picks up exactly where it left off after a restart instead of missing events or reprocessing the whole stream.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tradeoffs">
Four event subjects are declared (match finished, player registered, tournament updated, player ranking changed), but only two are actually published anywhere in the code today — match-finished and player-registered. The other two are consumed on the `social-bff` side but have no publish call site yet in `backend-nest`: real, honest evidence of designing the contract ahead of the implementation, not a hidden gap. Separately, the subject constants are duplicated by hand between the two services (there's no shared package), with a comment demanding manual sync — a real coupling risk that a shared library would remove.
</Callout>

<Callout type="result">
In production, finishing a match publishes `uft.match.finished` with the two teams, scores, and category, and `social-bff` turns that into a system-authored feed post (e.g. a result line, or "Empate" on a tie). Confirming a tournament registration publishes `uft.player.registered_tournament` once per roster player, and each becomes a personal activity post authored by that player's own account, not a generic system post.
</Callout>

<Callout type="lessons">
Fire-and-forget publish paired with a durable, explicitly-acked consumer is a good split for "the core product must never break because a side feature is down" — but declaring more of the event contract than you've wired up is only honest if you track which half is real, which is why this write-up says so directly instead of describing all four subjects as live. The manually-synced subject constants are the next piece of debt worth removing, ideally before a third consumer makes the sync problem worse.
</Callout>
