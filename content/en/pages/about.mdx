## From full-stack to backend

I started as a full-stack developer — React and Laravel on a hotel-booking platform, a Next.js/Stripe e-commerce module for a UK client, a full front-end rebuild of a legacy .NET education platform. Full-stack work taught me how a product holds together end to end: what the UI actually needs from an API, where a database schema constrains what a frontend can do, where deployment decisions show up as user-facing latency.

Building Ultimate Frisbee Tracker (UFT) pulled me toward the backend side of that equation. Realtime scorekeeping, tournament standings, and an event-driven layer on NATS JetStream are backend problems first — the frontend is a consumer of decisions made further down the stack. That's the work I want to keep doing.

## Engineering philosophy

> I enjoy building software that survives real users, not just demos. Every production issue is an opportunity to improve the system, not just a fire to put out. I'd rather ship something honest and incomplete than something polished and fake — most of what I actually know about engineering came from operating something in production, not from a tutorial. That's why this site documents decisions, not just outcomes.

## What I care about

- **Data models that match how the domain actually behaves**, not the easiest shape to query today.
- **Systems that stay correct under concurrency** — multiple scorekeepers, multiple devices, one source of truth.
- **Boring infrastructure.** Docker on a VPS over managed-platform lock-in, when I'm the one who has to operate it.
- **Documenting the decision, not just the code.** The Engineering and Case Studies sections exist because the reasoning behind a choice is worth more than the diff that implements it.

## Right now

I'm building and operating UFT in production — about 1,700 users tracked through Google Analytics, live since March 2025 — and writing up the engineering decisions behind it as I go.
